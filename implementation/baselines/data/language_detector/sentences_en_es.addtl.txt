en The group of stories brought together in this volume differ from legends because they have, with one exception, no core of fact at the
en centre, from myths because they make no attempt to personify or explain the forces or processes of nature, from fairy stories because they do not often bring on to the stage actors of a different nature
en from ours. They give full play to the fancy as in "A Child's Dream of a Star," "The King of the Golden River," "Undine," and "The Snow
en Image"; but they are not poetic records of the facts of life, attempts to shape those facts "to meet the needs of the imagination, the
en cravings of the heart." In the Introduction to the book of Fairy Tales in this series, those familiar and much loved stories which have been
en repeated to children for unnumbered generations and will be repeated to the end of time, are described as "records of the free and joyful
en play of the imagination, opening doors through hard conditions to the spirit, which craves power, freedom, happiness; righting wrongs, and
en redressing injuries; defeating base designs; rewarding patience and virtue; crowning true love with happiness; placing the powers of
en darkness under the control of man and making their ministers his servants." The stories which make up this volume are closer to
en experience and come, for the most part, nearer to the every-day happenings of life.
en A generation ago, when the noble activities of science and its inspiring discoveries were taking possession of the minds of men and
en revealing possibilities of power of which they had not dreamed, the prediction was freely made that poetry and fiction had had their day,
en and that henceforth men would be educated upon facts and get their inspirations from what are called real things. So engrossing and so
en marvellous were the results of investigation, the achievements of experiment, that it seemed to many as if the older literature of
en imagination and fancy had served its purpose as completely as alchemy, astrology, or chain armour.
en The prophecies of those fruitful years of research did not tell half the story of the wonderful things that were to be; the uses of
en electricity which are within easy reach for the most homely and practical purposes are as mysterious and magical as the dreams of the
en magicians. We are served by invisible ministers who are more powerful than the genii and more nimble than Puck. There has been a girdle
en around the world for many years; but there is good reason to believe that the time will come when news will go round the globe on waves of
en air. If we were not accustomed to ordering breakfast miles away from the grocer and the poulterer, we should be overcome with amazement
en every time we took up the telephone transmitter. Absolutely pure tones are now being made by the use of dynamos and will soon be sent into
en homes lying miles distant from the power house, so to speak, so that very sweet music is being played by arc lights.
en The anticipations of scientific men, so far as the uses of force are concerned, have been surpassed by the wonderful discoveries and
en applications of the past few years; but poetry and romance are not dead; on the contrary, they are more alive in the sense of awakening a
en wider interest than ever before in the history of writing. During the years which have been more fruitful in works of mechanical genius or
en dynamic energy, novels have been more widely distributed and more eagerly read than at any previous period. The poetry of the time, in
en the degree in which it has been fresh and vital, has been treated by newspapers as matter of universal interest.
en Men are born story-readers; if their interest subsides for the moment, or is absorbed by other forms of expression, it reasserts itself in
en due time and demands the old enchantment that has woven its spell over every generation since men and women reached an early stage of
en development. Barbarians and even savages share with the most highly civilised peoples this passion for fiction.
